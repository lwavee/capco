Deep Dive: Understanding Your General Liability Needs

How Much Coverage Do You Actually Need?

Most small businesses in California opt for a $1 million per occurrence / $2 million aggregate limit. This means the policy will pay up to $1M for a single claim and up to $2M total during the policy term. However, for high-risk industries like heavy construction or manufacturing, higher limits or an Excess Liability (Umbrella) policy may be recommended.

Factors That Influence Your Premium

When calculating your rate, insurance carriers look at several key data points:

  • Industry Risk: A roofing contractor will naturally pay more than an accountant due to the higher physical risk of the job.
  • Annual Revenue: Higher revenue often correlates with more client interactions and higher liability exposure.
  • Location: Operating in high-litigation areas or busy metropolitan hubs like Los Angeles can affect costs.
  • Claims History: A clean history of zero previous claims often qualifies you for "preferred" pricing and discounts.

General Liability vs. Professional Liability

It's a common misconception that GLI covers everything. General Liability covers physical accidents (slips, trips, property damage). Professional Liability (Errors & Omissions) covers financial losses caused by your professional advice or mistakes. Most California businesses benefit from having both.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does it cover my employees?

No. GLI covers third-party injuries. To protect your employees, you must have Workers' Compensation Insurance, which is legally required for all CA employers.

Can I get a Certificate of Insurance (COI) today?

Yes! Once your policy is bound and the first payment is made, we can issue your COI immediately so you can start working on that new contract.

Does GLI cover my tools and equipment?

Generally, no. You would need an Inland Marine (Tools & Equipment) floater to protect your physical gear from theft or damage.

Is "Commercial General Liability" different?

No, they are the same thing. CGL is simply the formal name used in legal and insurance documents.
